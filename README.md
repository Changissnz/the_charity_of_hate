## Death to the Meddler

The office smelled of old coffee and stale ambition. Fred stepped inside and closed the door behind him with the quiet care of a man who had learned long ago that loud entrances invited unwanted attention. He stood for a moment, taking in the room — the filing cabinets, the dusty blinds half-drawn against the afternoon light, and behind the desk, Bob.

Bob was the kind of man who looked permanent. Settled into his chair the way furniture settles into carpet, leaving impressions that outlast the object itself. He gestured to the seat across from him without looking up from his papers.

Fred sat.

"Your people have been pestering me," Fred said. No preamble. No pleasantries.

Bob set his papers down then. He folded his hands and gave Fred the look he reserved for clients and suspects alike — patient, unreadable, faintly amused.

"My name is Fred," Fred continued, as though the introduction belonged in the middle rather than the beginning. "I think it's important you know exactly who is sitting across from you."

"I know who you are," Bob said.

"Then you'll understand why I'm here in person." Fred leaned forward slightly. "The cops don't really work anymore. You know that. And the military —" he gave a short, humourless breath that wasn't quite a laugh — "corrupt, top to bottom. Which is why I'm giving you this courtesy face to face. Fair warning, man to man." His eyes were steady, unhurried. "I don't want to be harassed on the daily. If your firm continues down this road, it will suffer for it."

Bob listened the way a man listens when he has already made up his mind. He nodded at the appropriate moments, kept his expression neutral, and when Fred was finished, he thanked him for coming in.

Fred left.

Bob picked his papers back up.

---

What Fred didn't know — not at first — was the architecture behind the harassment. There was a contract. Quiet, unofficial in its tone but iron in its intent, drawn between the state department and Bob's firm. Certain important people had decided that Fred needed to be managed. Contained. And if the right pressure could be applied, incarcerated. Bob's firm was well-suited to such arrangements. They had the reach, the patience, and the institutional goodwill to make a man's life gradually, professionally unbearable.

So the surveillance continued. Watchers on corners. Phones that felt listened to. The subtle, suffocating sensation of being known.

Fred noticed all of it.

---

One week later, on a grey and unremarkable afternoon, Fred walked back into Bob's office.

He didn't sit down this time.

He drew the handgun from his jacket with the calm of a man fulfilling an obligation he had long since made peace with, and he emptied the magazine into Bob. The shots were loud in the small room, then the room was very quiet.

Fred stood there for a moment in the silence.

Then he produced a lighter, and he was thorough about the fire.

He walked out into the grey afternoon, and the building began to glow behind him.
