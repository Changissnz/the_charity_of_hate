## Death to the Meddler

The office smelled of old coffee and stale ambition. Fred stepped inside and closed the door behind him with the quiet care of a man who had learned long ago that loud entrances invited unwanted attention. He stood for a moment, taking in the room — the filing cabinets, the dusty blinds half-drawn against the afternoon light, and behind the desk, Bob.

Bob was the kind of man who looked permanent. Settled into his chair the way furniture settles into carpet, leaving impressions that outlast the object itself. He gestured to the seat across from him without looking up from his papers.

Fred sat.

"Your people have been pestering me," Fred said. No preamble. No pleasantries.

Bob set his papers down then. He folded his hands and gave Fred the look he reserved for clients and suspects alike — patient, unreadable, faintly amused.

"My name is Fred," Fred continued, as though the introduction belonged in the middle rather than the beginning. "I think it's important you know exactly who is sitting across from you."

"I know who you are," Bob said.

"Then you'll understand why I'm here in person." Fred leaned forward slightly. "The cops don't really work anymore. You know that. And the military —" he gave a short, humourless breath that wasn't quite a laugh — "corrupt, top to bottom. Which is why I'm giving you this courtesy face to face. Fair warning, man to man." His eyes were steady, unhurried. "I don't want to be harassed on the daily. If your firm continues down this road, it will suffer for it."

Bob listened the way a man listens when he has already made up his mind. He nodded at the appropriate moments, kept his expression neutral, and when Fred was finished, he thanked him for coming in.

Fred left.

Bob picked his papers back up.

---

What Fred didn't know — not at first — was the architecture behind the harassment. There was a contract. Quiet, unofficial in its tone but iron in its intent, drawn between the state department and Bob's firm. Certain important people had decided that Fred needed to be managed. Contained. And if the right pressure could be applied, incarcerated. Bob's firm was well-suited to such arrangements. They had the reach, the patience, and the institutional goodwill to make a man's life gradually, professionally unbearable.

So the surveillance continued. Watchers on corners. Phones that felt listened to. The subtle, suffocating sensation of being known.

Fred noticed all of it.

---

One week later, on a grey and unremarkable afternoon, Fred walked back into Bob's office.

He didn't sit down this time.

He drew the handgun from his jacket with the calm of a man fulfilling an obligation he had long since made peace with, and he emptied the magazine into Bob. The shots were loud in the small room, then the room was very quiet.

Fred stood there for a moment in the silence.

Then he produced a lighter, and he was thorough about the fire.

He walked out into the grey afternoon, and the building began to glow behind him.


## Reputation and Success

Tom had never been anyone's idea of a success story.

He had gone through college the way a stone goes through deep water — sinking quietly, unnoticed, touching nothing. No girlfriend. No close friends to speak of. He ranked at the bottom of his class, not for lack of intelligence, but for the particular kind of invisibility that attaches itself to certain men early in life and refuses to let go. He graduated into a world that received him with a shrug and moved on.

The commercial firms didn't call. Not one. He sent letters and résumés into that world and the world digested them silently, indifferently, the way it digests most things it doesn't need. Tom was, by almost every conventional measure, antithetical to the idea of a normal man — not through malice or strangeness, but through some quiet deficit of fortune that he had never been able to name or correct.

---

He ended up in a job that asked very little of him because it thought very little of him. Measly tasks. Errand work dressed up with a job title. He filed things and fetched things and answered to people who did not remember his name between interactions.

And then there were the others.

They were not a coordinated group. They had no shared agenda. They were simply the pompous and the comfortable — people whose success had curdled, over time, into a casual cruelty they no longer even recognised as cruelty. They condescended in passing. They dismissed him in meetings. They laughed at things adjacent to him, never quite at him directly, which was somehow worse. They treated him the way the well-situated often treat the invisible — as furniture, occasionally inconvenient.

This had been going on for years.

Tom catalogued it without meaning to. Each small humiliation filed itself away in him like sediment, layer by layer, building something dense and dark at the floor of who he was.

---

He lost it on an ordinary Tuesday.

There was no grand precipitating event. No final straw that history could point to and say — *there, that was the moment.* It was more that Tuesday arrived and Tom was simply finished. Whatever mechanism had been absorbing the weight of it all finally gave way, quietly and completely, the way old infrastructure fails — not with drama, but with the blunt exhaustion of something that has held on too long.

He came prepared.

When it was over, twenty people were dead. Fifteen of them were faces he knew. People who had chipped away at him across years and hallways and office kitchens — some of whom, he suspected, would not have been able to pick him out of a crowd even now. That fact had not escaped him.

The other five were simply present. Wrong place. Wrong Tuesday.

The noise of it filled the building and then stopped.

In the silence that followed, Tom sat down on the floor amid the ruin of what he had done. He didn't run. He had never planned to run. He had not, in the end, planned much beyond this room, this moment, this accounting.

He raised the gun one final time.

When the police arrived, they found twenty-one bodies.

---

In the days that followed, reporters would use words like *troubled* and *isolated.* Analysts would speak of warning signs. People who had barely registered Tom in life would speak at length about him in death — with authority, with gravity, with the comfortable expertise of those who had never once looked at him closely enough to see what was coming.

He had wanted, more than almost anything, to be seen.

In the end, the whole world saw him.

It was, in its own terrible and twisted way, the most successful thing he had ever done.

## How a Real Loser Got Made

Larry was the kind of smart that teachers notice and then forget to protect.

In grade school it had been obvious — the way he moved through problems, the connections he made before others had finished reading the question. His mind was quick and lateral and restless, the kind that thrives when nurtured and turns inward when it isn't. Some of his early teachers had marked him as exceptional. A few had even said so out loud.

Then the world got involved.

---

Being a minority was not, by itself, the obstacle. Larry understood that early. He watched peers who shared his background navigate the same institutions and emerge intact, even decorated. The difference, he came to understand, was money. It was the cushion of an affluent family — the tutors, the connections, the social fluency that comes from never having had to apologise for existing in a room. Those people could absorb the friction of the system because they arrived pre-buffered against it.

Larry arrived with nothing of the sort.

He came from the kind of background that made certain people in positions of authority quietly, methodically uncomfortable. And some of those people — the ones with the patience and the smallness for it — had decided that discomfort was actionable. That Larry's trajectory could be managed. Redirected. There were conversations Larry was never in the room for, calculus he was never shown, but whose results he felt everywhere he turned.

The logic, as best Larry could reconstruct it, was obscene in its pragmatism. The empire — and Larry had come to think of it that way, as a thing with appetite and intention — needed workers of every shape and colour. Not equals. Workers. And the most useful kind were those who had been broken just enough to be grateful for scraps, and just capable enough to perform. If you could engineer a man's diminishment carefully, you could own the labour of his recovery.

Larry was meant to be that kind of man.

---

University was where the engineering became visible.

His philosophy seminars should have been his arena. He was precise in argument, well-read beyond the syllabus, and unafraid of an uncomfortable conclusion — which is to say, he was exactly the kind of student that academic debate is supposed to reward. Instead, his professors sided consistently with his peers. Not always dramatically. Just reliably. The nod that went to someone else. The margin note that damned with faint praise. The grade that came back two points below what the same argument, in another student's handwriting, would have earned.

Larry tested this. He was methodical about it. He was not wrong.

The university was not, it became clear, in the business of endorsing Larry anywhere upward. It was in the business of processing him. Certifying him at a level that kept him available, kept him useful, kept him from asking too many questions from too high a vantage point.

He grew socially thin. The friendships that might have softened things didn't form — partly circumstance, partly the specific loneliness of knowing something others seem not to see. He became a man who observed more than he participated, and what he observed made him tired in a way that sleep did not fix.

---

The sympathies crept in gradually.

He didn't choose them so much as arrive at them through a process of elimination. The society he'd grown up in — the one that had offered him the language of meritocracy and then administered something else entirely — lost its claim on his loyalty degree by degree. He began reading things that western institutions would have called dangerous. He found, in the worldview of certain enemy organisations, a structural analysis that matched his experience more honestly than anything his professors had ever offered him.

He did not love these organisations. He was too clear-eyed for love. But he understood them. And understanding felt, after years of engineered confusion, like a form of sustenance.

---

He was back in his hometown when he made the decision.

How long it had been building is difficult to say. Perhaps it had always been there, waiting behind the intelligence and the patience and the long, disciplined habit of absorbing what the world dealt him. At some point the patience ran out and what was left underneath was cold and purposeful and done.

The high school reunion was well-attended. Sixty or so people who had grown up alongside Larry, most of whom had managed to forget him with impressive thoroughness. They stood in clusters with their drinks, performing the comfortable nostalgia of those whose memories had been kind to them.

Larry arrived with a black market AR-15 and an intention that had nothing nostalgic about it.

He did not linger in the doorway. He did not make a speech. He moved through the congregation with the focused efficiency of a man who had stopped being interested in being understood and started being interested only in being finished. By the time it was over, sixty people lay dead in the hall where they had once, years ago, been young together.

Larry walked outside.

The principal was standing near the entrance — frozen, useless, pale as old chalk. Perhaps the most fitting person in the world to be standing there in that moment, Larry thought. The administrator of his first diminishment. The first representative of the machine.

Larry looked at him for a moment.

Then he detonated the vest.

The blast took them both. The building shuddered. Glass moved outward in a slow, glittering exhale.

In the silence that followed, there was nothing left of Larry to bury that could be called whole.

---

He had been made, carefully and over many years, into something the empire could use.

In the end, he had declined the position.

## Joseph Kills Catholics

Joseph had not been raised to hate.

This was important to him, in the way that origins are always important to men who end up somewhere they never expected. His childhood home had been indifferent to religion — not hostile, not reverent, just quietly secular in the way of people who have more immediate things to worry about. Christianity was background noise. Catholics were neighbours, classmates, occasionally friends. Joseph had grown up among them without grievance.

That came later. That came gradually.

---

What Joseph observed, across the years, was not a single revelation but an accumulation of small, dispiriting data points that eventually achieved critical mass.

He watched Catholic peers and Catholic celebrities conduct their public lives with a specific variety of excess that fascinated him in its brazenness. Adultery had become, under certain authorities, something close to an institution — conducted openly, absorbed quietly, absolved efficiently. The infrastructure of forgiveness, Joseph noted, worked considerably faster for those with the right connections and the right cheque books.

He watched the torture of suspected terrorists get justified in careful language by men who wore their faith on their lapels and their brutality in their policy briefs. He watched minorities — people not unlike those he had grown up around — receive the cold end of organisations that spoke loudly of charity and practised something considerably narrower.

The political meddling was what darkened things most for Joseph. The reaching of institutional religion into civic life, into law, into the machinery of who got what and who got nothing. He found it insolent. That was the word he kept returning to. *Insolent.* The presumption of moral authority by people whose private lives, when the lights were low, looked nothing like the doctrine they administered to others.

And then there were the taxes. The fees and the indulgences — not medieval, exactly, but ancestrally familiar. The organisations he observed had developed their own informal economy of penalty and absolution. Joseph was not a wealthy man. The people levying these arrangements were. The fees that represented genuine hardship for someone like him represented, for them, a rounding error. They paid and continued unchanged, while men like Joseph were left exactly where they had always been: in the dust, and slightly poorer.

His prejudice grew large and dark and he kept it entirely to himself.

---

What broke something open in him was the discovery of what certain organisations had been doing with his unwitting cooperation.

He had been a pawn. He understood this slowly, then all at once, the way you understand you've been walking in the wrong direction — first a suspicion, then a sick certainty. There were people around him who had been using his proximity, his cluelessness, his clean record, as cover while they moved around him conducting capital crimes. A protection racket, scaffolded partly on his unknowing innocence. He had been useful to them in the way that furniture is useful — present, unquestioning, load-bearing.

When the full shape of it became clear to Joseph, something that had been dark in him went quiet and cold and operational.

---

He was deliberate about what came next.

He found a militant Catholic organisation — the kind that existed in the spaces between legitimate institution and outright criminal enterprise, the kind that sold things it shouldn't to people it shouldn't — and he purchased from them explosives and two automatic assault rifles. He was courteous in the transaction. He gave no indication of what he was purchasing them for.

The next day, he used them.

The bomb took the organisation's main building quickly. Twenty members died in the immediate blast — some mid-conversation, some mid-meal, some in the particular posture of men who believed themselves untouchable until the moment they weren't. The structure came down in sections, dust rising in a slow column visible from several streets away.

Ten members survived the initial blast and came out armed and furious.

Joseph was waiting.

The gunfight lasted hours. It moved through the surrounding streets in stages, grinding and attrition-based, neither side giving much ground quickly. Joseph was not a military man, but he was methodical and he had stopped caring about outcomes in the way that makes a man dangerous to engage. Five of the ten went down before Joseph did.

When he finally fell, it was without theatrics. He dropped, and the street went quiet, and the remaining five — the ones who had kept their heads and their nerve — regrouped in the chaos and fled. East, eventually. The Balkans. They did not look back.

---

Joseph left behind no manifesto, no recorded grievance, no final statement for the cameras. What he left behind was a ruin, a body count, and the particular silence of a man who had kept everything inside for so long that when it finally came out, it came out all at once and left nothing remaining.

He had not been raised to hate.

But he had been given, across a lifetime, an exceptionally thorough education in it.

## Hookup Culture and the Spy Industry

John had a threshold, like all men do. His just turned out to be lower than anyone around him had bothered to notice.

The public world had become, for him, an exhausting obstacle course of petty authority and organised shakedowns. The protection rackets were almost quaint in their brazenness — everybody knew, nobody said anything, and if you pushed back even slightly the cops arrived with their own brand of indulgence, fat and self-satisfied, hands out, eyes bored. John had learned to move through public spaces with the tight, careful energy of a man perpetually braced for an insult he couldn't afford to answer.

He stopped going out more than he had to.

---

Women had become a closed chapter.

It hadn't happened dramatically. It was more like a light going out in a room he'd been slowly spending less time in anyway. Since school ended, the social architecture that had at least provided proximity to women had dissolved, and John had not found a way to rebuild it in the adult world — a world that rewarded confidence he didn't have and money he couldn't accumulate and a specific performance of ease that struck him as fraudulent even in the men who managed it convincingly.

He watched his peers from a distance. Some of them moved through hookup culture like it was their birthright — spending lavishly, carelessly, on experiences and appearances and the rotating theatre of modern courtship. John understood, without bitterness at first and then with plenty of it, where that money came from. Inheritance. Class. The quiet subsidy of families who had arrived early enough to own things. No fair system — no system that actually measured effort or intelligence or simple human decency — would have granted those men what they had. It had been handed to them before they were old enough to have done anything to deserve it or squander it.

John had not been handed anything.

---

There had been one relationship. High school. A girl he had genuinely cared for in the uncomplicated, earnest way that is only really possible before the world has finished with you.

She was a closet lesbian.

He found out after. He was never entirely sure how long she had known, or what exactly their relationship had represented to her — cover, perhaps, or genuine confusion, or something in between that didn't have a clean name. It didn't much matter. What mattered was the particular species of humiliation involved: to discover that the one intimacy you had managed had been, in some fundamental sense, an exercise in mutual misdirection. That the closest he had come to being chosen had not quite been that.

He did not blame her loudly or publicly. He simply closed the door on that part of himself and did not reopen it.

---

The economy did the rest.

The energy crises had arrived in waves, each one shaving a little more off the margin that people like John lived within. Then the AI displacement — mass unemployment arriving not like a storm but like a tide, slow and indifferent and total. Whole categories of work simply stopped requiring human beings. John moved between whatever remained: the jobs that paid poorly because they could, that demanded everything because there was always someone more desperate waiting behind you in line.

He held them down and then didn't. Held the next one and then didn't. The money was never enough and the hours were always too many and the men above him in these arrangements had the specific cruelty of people who know they are replaceable but have not yet been replaced.

---

He saved carefully, the way a man saves when saving is the only form of agency left available to him.

When he had enough, he purchased a Remington shotgun from a private seller. He drove home with it in the trunk and sat in his apartment for a while, not doing anything, just sitting.

That night he went to her place first.

She answered the door and he killed her in the doorway. He did not say anything before he did it. There was nothing that needed saying that the act itself didn't say more plainly.

Then he drove to the strip club on the edge of town — the one he had passed a hundred times, lit up pink and gold, full of the noise and the flesh of a world that had never once made room for him at the table. He went inside and he killed everyone in the building, working through the rooms with the grim patience that had, it turned out, always been his most consistent quality.

Then he sat down, and he did not get up again.

---

When police arrived, they moved through the building with weapons drawn and found no one to arrest. The reports filed afterward were blunt in their accounting. The shooter was identified. His history was reconstructed from records and the sparse testimony of people who had known him at a remove.

The reports noted, without particular elaboration, that John had been a virgin. That the job market had not treated him fairly. That his peers had not treated him fairly. These facts were recorded in the same clinical register as the ballistics and the body count, filed alongside them as context, or perhaps as explanation, or perhaps as neither — just data, inert and enormous, sitting in a folder that would eventually be closed and shelved and forgotten.

John would have found that fitting.

He had been filed away his whole life.

## That One Asian Immigrant

Nobody ever really knew what Tran Chang Hao Tsien had lived through before she arrived.

She didn't speak of it. Whatever had happened to her in Asia — whatever had shaped the architecture of her interior world before America got involved — remained sealed behind a silence she had never invited anyone to breach. Her children had stopped trying to understand it and started simply enduring the results.

The results were considerable.

---

Tran was a difficult woman in the specific way of people who have decided, at some point they can no longer identify, that the world is an adversarial place requiring constant management. She had no patience — not as a personality quirk but as a governing philosophy. Things were to be done her way, at her pace, according to her judgment, and deviation was not a difference of opinion but a personal affront. She passed verdict on strangers without solicitation, cataloguing their life choices in the language of shame, then turning to her children and telling them that back in Asia, such behaviour would be a disgrace. The irony of rendering these judgments from a kitchen in suburban America appeared to escape her entirely.

She believed in corporal punishment the way some people believe in God — not as a last resort but as a first principle, a foundational truth about how order is maintained and character is built. She spoke approvingly of state-sanctioned torture with the calm of someone discussing a sensible policy position.

Her adult children had learned to make themselves small in her presence. Not out of respect — that had eroded across years of accumulated grievance — but out of the exhausted pragmatism of people who have stopped expecting the situation to improve and started simply managing its costs.

---

Lee was the oldest.

The Covid years had taken things from him that he hadn't fully inventoried yet. Work, mostly. Stability. The particular forward momentum that a man in his thirties requires to feel like his life is going somewhere rather than simply continuing. When it collapsed, he moved back home because there was nowhere else to go, and home meant his mother's house, and his mother's house meant all of this.

He had come back from the pandemic years with a grudge that had several distinct targets.

There were the Americans — a category he had refined, over time, into those specifically responsible for what he'd witnessed: the pandemic-era fraud, the welfare manipulation, the opportunism dressed up as necessity. And there was the discrimination. The way Chinese faces had become, during those years, a surface onto which certain people projected their fear and their anger and their need for a legible enemy. Lee found this obscene. He was not without his own complicated feelings about the Chinese — he carried those contradictions without resolving them — but the unfairness of it still registered clearly. Hating something yourself did not mean you were willing to watch others do it.

---

The paranoia settled in gradually.

He began to feel observed. Not in the dramatic way of a man who has broken from reality, but in the quiet, grinding way of someone whose circumstances have given him genuine reason to be watchful — and who can no longer reliably distinguish between what is real and what his exhausted mind is constructing. People's behaviour toward him had shifted, or seemed to have. Small signals. Tonal changes. The way a room adjusted when he entered it.

His younger siblings had changed too. Or had been changed. He could sense it in their interactions — a new guardedness, a quality of carefully managed neutrality that felt less like indifference and more like briefing. Someone had talked to them about him. Someone had put them in a loop he hadn't been invited into. The whole atmosphere of it had a familiar institutional smell — organised, layered, deniable. It reminded him of nothing so much as the Catholic machinery he'd observed operating through the earlier chapters of his life: the quiet inquisition, the managed consensus, the sense of judgment being administered from somewhere just out of sight.

---

He spent several months in the orbit of a Hispanic gang that operated in the neighbourhood. They were not ideological people. They were practical people, and practically speaking, they had access to things Lee needed.

He acquired the firearms without difficulty.

---

He began at home.

He killed his entire family in the house where he had grown up, in the rooms where his mother had administered her particular brand of order. He moved through it methodically, and when it was done he left without looking back at what he had made of the place.

He drove to the police station.

Seven officers died before Lee did. The gunfight was brief and one-sided in its conclusion if not in its duration. When Lee went down, it was in the parking lot, under a sky that was beginning to lighten at the edges toward morning.

---

The FBI found the manifesto during the investigation. It had been drafted carefully, revised multiple times — the work of a man who had wanted to be understood even if he had stopped expecting to be.

Its argument, stripped to its frame, was this: America was a country of last resort. It collected the persecuted and the low-born and the desperate from everywhere else in the world, processed them through its machinery, and called the result opportunity. What it actually produced, in Lee's accounting, was a sedentary society — one so habituated to its own dysfunction that it had lost the capacity for honest self-examination, let alone correction. He had taken drastic action, he wrote, not because he believed it would fix anything, but because he was no longer willing to participate in the collective pretence that things were fine or fixable through normal means.

He had wanted to send a message.

Whether anyone received it in the spirit intended was, by the time the report was filed, no longer something Lee was available to assess.

## The Terrible Opportunities

America had never quite figured out what it believed in.

Not at the foundational level. Not in the place where a society's actual operating values live, beneath the documents and the speeches and the mythology. It believed, officially, in many things. In practice it believed in price — the unspoken, unmarked price attached to everything it nominally offered.

The high-paying job came with expectations designed by people who had never needed to sleep. The stable job came with coworkers who had long since made peace with their own mediocrity and resented anyone who hadn't. The brand name college came with professors so thoroughly marinated in their own theoretical frameworks that education had become a secondary concern, somewhere behind ideology and ego and the maintenance of departmental politics. The pretty woman — and Brad had learned this one the hard way, more than once — came with her own complicated entanglements, her own institutional loyalties, her own role in networks of information and surveillance that a man like Brad was never meant to understand until it was too late to matter.

Everything had a hidden cost. The country had simply stopped advertising it.

---

Brad had served.

He had been good at it, which was the uncomplicated part of his military history. His marksmanship scores had been exceptional — the kind of numbers that got noticed, that earned a certain quiet respect from instructors who didn't give it easily. He was disciplined, physically capable, and possessed of the particular mental steadiness that accurate shooting requires: the ability to be, in the moment of execution, entirely still inside.

The dishonorable discharge was the complicated part.

His superior officer had said something — done something — that Brad had absorbed up to a point and then had not. The details of what preceded the incident were not reflected in the official record with anything approaching fairness, and Brad had long since stopped expecting fairness from official records. What the record showed was that he had beaten the man with his bare hands to within a clinical distance of death, and that this was not the kind of thing the institution was structured to contextualise or excuse.

He came home carrying that. On top of everything else, he carried that.

---

The crypto scheme was almost funny in retrospect, if you had the stomach for that kind of humour.

An AI-driven platform, slick and algorithmic and staffed, at the human-facing end, by people who had the specific fluency of those who have run the same con enough times to have made it feel like a genuine conversation. Brad lost money he couldn't afford to lose. More than that, he lost the thing the money had represented — the possibility of a margin, a buffer, a small piece of financial ground he could stand on without it shifting under him.

In the aftermath of that, he started reading things he might not have otherwise read. The anti-Semitic organisations he encountered were not subtle in their recruitment of grievance. They had long practice at finding men in Brad's particular condition — duped, humiliated, looking for a framework that could absorb his anger and give it a named target — and offering them a cosmology of blame that was internally consistent if entirely wrong. Brad did not embrace it wholeheartedly. He was too clear-eyed for wholesale adoption of anyone's ideology. But he listened. He absorbed. He let it sit alongside everything else he was carrying.

---

The church had been, for most of his life, a place of at least nominal comfort.

It stopped being that.

He watched the collection plates go around with the regularity of a subscription service and the emotional leverage of an organisation that had learned, over centuries, how to make financial obligation feel like spiritual duty. He watched his church leaders perform their authority — buffoonish in their self-importance, insulated from consequence by the deference of congregations that had been trained since childhood to extend them benefit of the doubt they had not always earned. The gap between the sermon and the man delivering it had become, for Brad, impossible to look past.

He sat in the pews and he calculated.

---

He brought five handguns and a thousand rounds of ammunition.

The church was full. Sunday morning, the parking lot crowded, the congregation settled into the comfortable rituals of people who have attended the same service so many times that the movements have become automatic. The music, the call and response, the rustle of programs.

Brad had been among them many times.

He was methodical about it. He had been trained to be methodical, and that training had not left him even when everything else the military had given him had been officially revoked. He moved through the building with the stillness he had always possessed under pressure — the same stillness that had made his marksmanship scores exceptional, now applied to a purpose that the men who had trained him could not have imagined and probably, in their private moments, would not have wanted to.

Two hundred people died before Brad turned the last of his ammunition on himself.

The building went quiet in the particular way of places where very loud things have just happened. Outside, the Sunday morning continued for a moment, oblivious — birds, traffic, the mundane persistence of a world that had not yet received the news.

---

In the investigations that followed, analysts would reconstruct the timeline of Brad's radicalisation with the confident hindsight of people working backward from a known destination. They would identify the discharge, the financial loss, the ideological drift, the accumulated institutional disappointments. They would assemble these facts into a narrative with a shape that made sense.

What none of the reports would quite capture was the texture of it from the inside. The long, grinding process of a man discovering, one terrible opportunity at a time, that the country he had served and the institutions he had trusted had been running a different set of accounts than the ones they showed him — and that the discrepancy, by the time he understood its full dimensions, was not something he was willing to simply absorb and move on from.

He had been still, for a very long time.

Then he wasn't.

## The Ex Software Engineer

Thomas had done everything they told him to do.

That was the part that stuck. He had followed the prescribed path with the diligence of someone who still believed, at the time, that the path led somewhere. Four years of undergraduate work — the assignments, the exams, the group projects with people he didn't like, the professors who graded on curves that seemed to bend toward everyone but him. He came out the other side with a degree and the reasonable expectation that the degree meant something.

It didn't mean what he thought it meant.

---

The job applications went out and the responses came back thin and noncommittal, or didn't come back at all. He watched the industry from the outside with the focused attention of someone trying to reverse-engineer a system that had excluded him, looking for the variable he had failed to account for.

What he found, or what he told himself he found, curdled in him quickly.

The coding bootcamp graduates — people who had spent a few thousand dollars and a few months on accelerated programs that Thomas privately considered academically embarrassing — were getting hired ahead of him. The industry, as Thomas observed it through the narrowing lens of his resentment, had its own sorting logic that bore little relationship to the meritocracy it advertised. He watched DEI initiatives move candidates he considered less qualified into positions he had applied for. He watched the technical architecture of the industry get built and maintained by Asian engineers while the social and corporate layer above them filled with other demographics entirely. He mapped these observations onto his failure and drew lines between them that felt, to him, like honest analysis.

They were not honest analysis. They were the geometric constructions of a wounded man who needed the shape of his exclusion to have clean edges. But Thomas had stopped being interested in the distinction between what was true and what was useful.

---

His contempt spread outward from the industry into its mythology.

The names that were held up as monuments — Terence Tao, Einstein, the canonical geniuses of mathematics and physics and computer science — he had come to spit on them. Not because he doubted their intelligence, but because their elevation had become, in his reading of things, a mechanism of humiliation. A standard held up to make ordinary men feel the precise dimensions of their inadequacy. Genius, as institutionally celebrated, was a weapon. He was done pretending to admire it.

The advertisers and the journalists he hated with a more focused contempt. The ones who cycled through their trend pieces — *software is dead, learn a trade, blue collar is back, the coding era is over* — as though the market were a weather system and they were providing a public service by reporting it, rather than actively shaping the perceptions of hundreds of thousands of people trying to make decisions about their lives. They had told a generation that software was the future. Now they were telling another generation it was over. Thomas had been caught in the gap between these two announcements, and no one was going to write a piece about that.

---

His Army contact was a man whose name Thomas had stopped using in any context that might be recorded. The man was capable and ideologically motivated in ways that Thomas found useful without fully endorsing. He was on several lists maintained by federal agencies, which Thomas knew and had decided not to care about.

They planned it together over several weeks. The social media company had come to represent, for Thomas, the concentrated essence of everything he held in contempt — the algorithm-driven manipulation, the advertiser relationships, the cultivated illusion of connection masking a machine built entirely around attention extraction and data harvesting. It had also, specifically, rejected his job application twice.

The dynamite arrangement was his contact's area of expertise. Thomas deferred to him on the technical specifics and focused on the logistics. They drove over on a Wednesday morning — midweek, maximum occupancy, the building full of people whose LinkedIn profiles Thomas had spent an evening reading with a feeling he couldn't entirely name.

The detonation took out the office and roughly half the people inside it. The building came apart in sections, the kind of structural failure that looks, from a distance, almost orderly.

In the aftermath, while the dust was still moving, Thomas turned and shot his contact twice. He had decided, at some point in the planning, that this was how it would end — that he would not share the conclusion of this with anyone, would not be appended to another man's story or another man's ideology. Whatever this was, it was his.

He turned the gun on himself before the first sirens were audible.

---

The federal investigation that followed was extensive. Thomas's digital history was reconstructed in detail — the job applications, the forum posts, the gradual ideological drift charted across years of search history and private messages. His contact's background filled several additional files, the suspected domestic terrorism charges now moot and formally noted as such.

Thomas left no manifesto. He had written several drafts and deleted them all. None of them had said what he wanted to say in a way that satisfied him, and he had decided, in the end, that a man who had spent years being misread by systems that were supposed to understand him had no particular reason to believe a written statement would fare any better.

He had made his point the only way that felt, to him, legible.

Whether anyone read it correctly was no longer his problem.

## The Slav That Hated Johnson Brandon

Nikolov Vasputinovich had not always been political.

There had been a time, not so long ago, when the abstractions of geopolitics had felt genuinely abstract — something that happened in briefing rooms and news crawls and the op-ed pages of papers he skimmed without much investment. He had come to America with the pragmatic orientation of a man who wanted to work and be left alone, and for a while, that had seemed like a reasonable arrangement.

Then he began paying attention.

---

Johnson Brandon was, by official designation, a government bureaucrat. By functional description he was something closer to an atmosphere — a persistent, institutional presence that seemed to be everywhere Nikolov looked whenever the subject of Russia arose, which was constantly and with a consistency that Nikolov had begun to find structurally suspicious.

Brandon blamed Russians with the fluency of a man who had found a groove and saw no reason to leave it. Cyberattacks, election interference, disinformation, destabilisation — the list was long and Brandon was tireless. He appeared on panels and in reports and in the background of news segments, always with the same measured authority, always with Russia in the position of prime mover.

What enraged Nikolov was not the criticism itself. He was not naive about his country of origin or the behaviour of its government. What enraged him was the selective architecture of it — the way Brandon and those like him trained the aperture so precisely on one source of blame that everything else fell outside the frame. There were Americans causing serious damage to other Americans through fraud and manipulation and institutional corruption, and that damage was largely invisible in the narrative that Brandon and his colleagues maintained. The Russians were the explanation for everything. The explanation for everything else was, apparently, no one's department.

---

Nikolov watched his peers get arrested.

One after another — men he knew, men he had shared meals with and complained to about the weather and the cost of things — caught up in cybercrimes that Nikolov did not believe, in several cases, they had actually committed. The setups were not always elegant. Sometimes they were barely coherent. But they were effective, which was the only quality that mattered in an environment where the conclusion had been decided in advance and the evidence was being assembled to support it rather than to discover the truth.

Each arrest tightened something in Nikolov. He became watchful in a way that is difficult to sustain without cost — the constant low-level expenditure of attention that hypervigilance requires, the way it hollows out the bandwidth available for anything else. He slept badly. He trusted almost no one. He spent long hours reading and constructing and reconstructing the architecture of what he believed was being done to people like him, in this country, under the cover of a narrative that Brandon and his apparatus maintained with professional dedication.

His conclusions, by the end of this process, had traveled a considerable distance from where he had started.

---

The Vietnamese restaurant was called something cheerful in two languages. It sat on a commercial block with the comfortable permanence of a neighbourhood institution, the kind of place with laminated menus and regular customers and a specific smell of broth and star anise that hit you when the door opened.

Nikolov went on a Tuesday.

Fifty people died inside. He moved through the restaurant with the blank efficiency of a man who had, in the privacy of his own reasoning, already completed the moral accounting and found it settled. He did not rush. He did not speak. When it was over he sat down at one of the tables and waited for the police to arrive, and when they did he did not resist.

The manifesto was in his jacket pocket, folded carefully.

---

The document was several pages long, dense with the specific texture of a man who has spent too long alone with his own conclusions. The FBI translated and analysed it. Its reasoning, such as it was, ran like this:

He had been made to suffer for his identity. Made to carry the weight of a national association he had not chosen and could not shed, in a country that had decided what he was before he opened his mouth. If that was the operating logic of the world he inhabited — that identity was liability, that origin was guilt, that the circumstances of your birth determined the treatment you received — then he saw no principled reason why that logic should apply only to him.

The Vietnamese at that restaurant, he wrote, were not innocent bystanders in a neutral world. They were participants in the same system of identity and association. He constructed an elaborate framework around the restaurant's alleged ownership — Southern Vietnamese connections, French Catholic money, the whole apparatus of what he called turncoat collaboration — that justified, in his accounting, their inclusion in his grievance. He invoked Ho Chi Minh with the confidence of a man who has claimed an ideological inheritance he has no real right to, arguing that the revolutionary would have approved of striking at this particular target.

The reasoning was the reasoning of a man who had taken a genuine grievance — real surveillance, real scapegoating, real institutional bias — and followed it into territory where it no longer had any legitimate claim. He had started with something true and traveled until he reached something monstrous, and somewhere along the way had lost the ability to feel the distance between the two.

The FBI filed the manifesto alongside the forensic reports.

Johnson Brandon issued no public comment on the incident.

He rarely did, about specific cases. He preferred the broader frame.

## Populists, Poppy, and Mass Shootings

America had always had appetites larger than its capacity for consequence.

This had been true for a long time, but the particular era in which Hector Bannon came of age had made it newly visible — stripped away the insulation of prosperity that had previously allowed the country to indulge itself without having to look too directly at what the indulgence cost. The narcotics appetite was the most literal expression of it. Opioids had moved through the country's working class like a slow tide, and when the pharmaceutical version got harder to access, the market simply adjusted — poppy-derived alternatives arriving through supply chains that were, in their own way, more reliable than anything the legitimate economy was offering.

The debt was everywhere. Not the manageable, purposeful kind that implies a future transaction — the grinding, structurally permanent kind that had become simply the condition of existence for the majority of working Americans. You carried it the way you carried your body. It was just there, every morning, waiting.

Friendship, Hector had observed, had quietly become a class amenity. The rich had it because they had the time and the venues and the shared cushion of financial security that allows people to be generous with each other. The normies — the solidly employed, the conventionally stable — had it because their lives had enough predictable shape to sustain ongoing social arrangements. Everyone else was too tired and too stressed and too financially precarious to maintain the sustained mutual investment that friendship actually requires. You could see it in the dropout rates from every kind of civic and social organisation. Community was expensive. Most people couldn't afford it anymore.

---

The corporations had finished the job that deindustrialisation had started.

In an earlier era, multinationals had at least maintained the pretence of national loyalty — headquartered somewhere, employing someone, participating in the civic fiction of mutual obligation between capital and country. That pretence had dissolved. The fraying of international agreements, the breakdown of the trade architecture that had governed the previous decades, had liberated corporations from even the performative version of national allegiance. They moved capital and contracts and manufacturing capacity with the unsentimental fluency of water finding the lowest ground, and what they left behind in places like Hector's town was the aftermath — shuttered storefronts, part-time work dressed up as opportunity, the hollowed-out infrastructure of communities that had organised themselves around industries that were no longer there.

Some of these corporations, Hector had come to believe, were not merely negligent. They were operational covers. The line between multinational logistics and paramilitary supply chain was, in certain documented cases, thinner than the official record liked to acknowledge. He had seen enough to believe this was not paranoia.

Populism had risen to fill the vacuum, as it always does. It offered the satisfactions of a named enemy and a legible narrative, which were considerable satisfactions when the alternative was the grinding abstraction of systemic failure. Hector called himself a populist without embarrassment. It was as accurate a label as any.

---

The heroin trade was, in its own way, the most honest economy operating in America during those years.

Demand was enormous and consistent — the kind of demand that recessions don't dent because the product is addressing a need that economic conditions only intensify. Hector had not entered the trade ideologically. He had entered it practically, the way men enter most things that fall outside the law — because the legal alternatives had closed off, one by one, and this one remained open and profitable and, with the right structure, manageable.

The network he helped facilitate was sophisticated in its layering. Turkish connections handled significant portions of the supply chain at the source end — the poppy-growing regions feeding into processing routes that moved product westward through channels that had been operational, in various configurations, for decades. Vietnamese intermediaries managed portions of the domestic distribution, working through community structures that were largely invisible to federal surveillance. Filipino and Chinese partners provided additional insulation at the financial layer — the movement of money through enough legitimate-seeming transactions that the trail became genuinely difficult to follow without extensive resources and time.

The architecture had been designed with Interpol and the federal agencies specifically in mind. Each layer knew only what it needed to know. Each nationality of intermediary provided plausible deniability at a different point in the chain. The whole structure was, in the abstract, an impressive piece of operational engineering — the kind that suggested someone, somewhere in its design, had significant experience with exactly the kind of enforcement they were trying to evade.

Then the deals fell through.

Not all at once. It happened in stages, the way complex systems fail — a weak point here, a compromised contact there, a transaction that didn't clear and a partner who stopped returning communications. The Turkish supply line developed problems that were never fully explained. The domestic distribution network contracted suddenly when two of the Vietnamese intermediaries were picked up on unrelated charges and the ripple effect moved through the structure faster than anyone had anticipated. Money that was supposed to move didn't move. Product that was supposed to arrive didn't arrive.

Hector absorbed the losses with the resignation of a man who had always known, on some level, that this would not end cleanly. He had made money while it worked. He had also made enemies — partners who held him partially responsible for the collapse of arrangements they had relied upon, whose losses were not abstract and whose memories were long.

He did not discuss this with anyone.

---

The Irish Americans were a separate grievance, older and more personal.

Hector had watched them operate in his town across years of increasingly focused attention. What he saw — or what he told himself he saw — was a community more interested in the acquisition and maintenance of political power than in the actual exercise of any craft or the building of any durable thing. They were gamblers, in his assessment. Men who treated civic life as a card game, accumulating influence through relationship and favours and the particular Irish-American fluency for institutional navigation that had been refined across generations of machine politics. They built power structures rather than things. They secured positions rather than perfecting skills.

He found this, in the context of everything else, intolerable.

The pub was warm and loud on a wintry night, full of the specific comfortable noise of people who feel at home in a place. Hector walked in with the short sword he had purchased through the Amazon marketplace — an item listed under historical reproduction, delivered in three days, no questions — and he moved through the room with a cold and deliberate thoroughness.

The slashing attack killed everyone inside.

When it was finished, Hector stood in the ruin of the warm room and the noise was entirely gone. He looked at what he had done with the expression of a man completing an accounting he had been working on for a very long time.

Then he disemboweled himself — an act that managed, in its specific chosen form, to be the most deliberate thing he had done all evening.

He dropped in the sawdust and the silence.

Outside, it was still winter.

## Respects Paid, After Inflation

Song Ho-Chun Park was, by any honest measure, a remarkable man.

His scientific knowledge was deep and self-directed — the kind built by someone who reads not because a curriculum demands it but because the alternative is to leave questions unanswered, which he found genuinely intolerable. His martial training had given him a physical discipline that complemented the mental one, a body that moved with the same precision he applied to problems. He had arrived in the United States at twenty with these things intact, and with the reasonable if naive expectation that they would count for something.

They counted for less than he had anticipated.

---

The English was the first obstacle and the most persistent one.

He had learned it academically, which meant he could read it and write it and construct grammatically sound sentences with it, but when it came out of his mouth it came out filtered through the phonological architecture of Korean in a way he could not fully control and could not make himself stop hearing. The accent was heavy. And there was something in the particular register of it — some quality of pitch or cadence or the specific way certain vowels landed — that American ears processed in a way Park had not been prepared for.

Men who sounded like that, in the American social taxonomy, were coded in a specific way. The gay white men of his neighbourhood had apparently decided he was a version of them — exotic, available, interesting in the way that foreignness makes things briefly interesting — and approached him with a directness that Park found not flattering but violating. He was not what they saw when they looked at him. He did not know how to explain this in a way that his accent would not undermine. He became quiet instead.

The blonde women laughed. Not always cruelly, not always consciously — sometimes it was just the reflex laughter of people who find something unexpected and respond before they've decided whether it's funny. But it happened consistently enough that Park had catalogued it, the way he catalogued most things that caused him sustained pain, until the catalogue itself became a weight he carried everywhere.

He stopped speaking in public unless absolutely necessary.

The silence grew.

---

His mother, contacted by a psychiatrist during his mid-twenties, had been instructed to ensure he took his medication. The diagnosis was clinical, the prescription specific. Park took the antipsychotics irregularly — sometimes for stretches of weeks, then not at all, in the pattern of someone who resents the implication of what the pills represent more than he fears what happens without them. During the periods off the medication his thinking developed a quality of heightened connection, a sense that things were more meaningfully related than they appeared on the surface. He found this clarity seductive even as some distant, functioning part of him recognised it as a symptom.

He was bullied throughout his twenties in the specific way available to a man in his circumstances — not physically, mostly, but socially and professionally, in the accumulated small violences of being consistently misread, condescended to, propositioned when he wanted to be taken seriously, and laughed at when he wanted to be heard.

The martyrdom complex, as the psychiatrist had noted in records that would later be recovered and analysed, had been developing for some time. The sense of a destiny thwarted. Of a value unrecognised. Of a specific greatness that the world had failed, through its own corruption and superficiality, to acknowledge.

---

He was alone on the evening it broke open.

The television was on and the media was doing what media does — cycling through its roster of the celebrated, the successful, the institutionally approved faces of achievement. Terence Tao appeared on the screen. Mathematician. Prodigy. Widely described as one of the greatest mathematical minds alive.

Park watched for a moment.

Then something that had been under pressure for a long time found its release point.

He pulled out the Desert Eagle and fired into the screen repeatedly, the shots loud in the small apartment, the television dying in stages. He stood in the ringing silence afterward and heard himself say it — *I deserve to be like Terence Tao* — and then heard what came after, the thing that had apparently been underneath the admiration all along, the resentment that had been quietly composting in the dark: *I am Korean. Tao is Chinese. Chinese. Too many Chinese in this world. Not valuable.*

The logic was not logic. It was pain dressed in the clothing of ideology — the oldest substitution there is.

He reloaded.

---

Chinatown was twenty minutes away.

He moved through it with the Desert Eagle and killed twenty ethnic Chinese, two Arabs who were present by the accident of geography, and one Black man who had been in the wrong intersection at the wrong moment. The shooting had the quality of all such acts at the granular level — chaotic, loud, over faster than the survivors could later believe, leaving behind a stillness that felt obscene given what had preceded it.

Park drove from Chinatown to the community college.

The explosive vest took out a section of the main building's facade, leaving the gaping structural wound that the subsequent news footage would replay for days — a hole in the brick and glass that looked, from certain angles, almost purposeful, as though something had been trying to get out from inside.

Park did not survive the detonation.

---

The manifesto was recovered intact.

It was the document of a man who had spent years constructing, in private, a narrative of thwarted greatness that had gradually consumed the more grounded parts of his self-understanding. He wrote about his scientific gifts with the authority of someone who genuinely possessed them, which made the document more unsettling than the raving of someone entirely detached from reality. The gifts were real. The conclusion he had drawn from their non-recognition — that violence was the appropriate response, that others must pay the price of his obscurity — was the place where the illness and the pain and the years of accumulated humiliation had taken him somewhere he could not find his way back from.

He had wanted to be Terence Tao.

He had ended up in a federal evidence file instead, cross-referenced with a body count and a psychiatric history and the records of a prescription his mother had tried, inadequately, to ensure he filled.

The inflation he had felt — of his own worth, of his own destiny, of the debt the world owed him — had compounded across years until it could no longer be contained.

Respects, of a kind, had been paid.

Not the kind he had wanted.

## The Gossip Industry

The Information Age had promised something.

That was the part worth remembering, before accounting for what it had actually delivered. The promise had been genuine in its original architecture — access, democratisation, the levelling of the information landscape so that knowledge was no longer the exclusive property of institutions and gatekeepers. Anyone could know anything. Anyone could speak. The walls were coming down.

What nobody had fully modelled was what happens when everyone speaks at once, and when speaking becomes commercially incentivised, and when the machinery built to distribute information turns out to be optimised not for truth or depth or even accuracy but for engagement — which is a clinical word for the particular neurological reward that outrage and gossip and the confirmation of existing beliefs reliably produces in the human brain.

The quality diluted. This was not an opinion; it was measurable, if you had the patience to measure it. The signal-to-noise ratio shifted year by year until the noise was the dominant experience and the signal required serious excavation to locate. Everyone had a platform. Everyone had a viewpoint to push and an audience, however small, to push it to. The propaganda was not centralised anymore — it was distributed, personalised, ambient. You swam in it. It got into everything.

Ronald had been swimming in it for years and he was exhausted in the specific way of a man who has been trying to find solid ground in water.

---

He had not had a real conversation in a long time.

This was the thing that he found difficult to explain to anyone — partly because the people he might have explained it to were themselves too saturated in the information noise to slow down enough to listen, and partly because the absence of real conversation had, over time, eroded some of the social fluency required to initiate one. He had become quiet in the way that isolated people become quiet — not from preference but from the gradual atrophying of the muscles required for genuine exchange.

Everything around him was talking. Nothing around him was saying anything.

---

The hometown had not improved in his absence.

It had the specific stagnation of places that the economy had decided were not worth the energy of transformation — not dramatic enough in their decline to attract crisis funding, not strategically located enough to attract investment, just persistently depressed in the way of a patient whose condition has stabilised at a level that is technically not dying but bears little resemblance to health. The drugs were woven into the social fabric the way they always are in places with no competing industry — filling the void left by purposeful work, providing the only reliable form of relief available on short notice and without prerequisites.

Ronald moved through his days with the tight economy of someone perpetually short of money and unwilling to show how much it costs him. Always somewhat broke. Always making the small calculations that broke people make — which bill waits, which purchase can be deferred, which necessity gets reclassified as a luxury this week.

And then there was the feeling of being watched.

He couldn't source it cleanly. It arrived in the texture of interactions — the way certain people in certain places seemed to track him with a fraction more attention than casual observation required. The way conversations seemed to reference things that he hadn't told anyone. The way the town's gossip network, which was extensive and efficient in the way that small town gossip networks always are, seemed to have a Ronald-shaped thread running through it that he could feel being pulled without being able to locate the hand doing the pulling.

Whether this was real or the product of an increasingly strained mind processing an environment that had given it genuine reasons for hypervigilance, Ronald could not determine. He had stopped trusting his own assessment of the boundary between those two things.

---

The shopping centre was the town's main civic gathering point — the agora of a community that had nothing else to organise itself around. It had the particular atmosphere of such places: the fluorescent permanence, the recycled air, the mix of people who were there because they needed something and people who were there because they had nowhere else to be.

Ronald went on a Thursday.

He did the shooting with the methodical blankness of a man who has moved beyond the emotional register of what he is doing and into something that functions more like procedure. The details were what they always were — the noise, the movement, the sudden restructuring of a space from mundane to catastrophic. When it was over, people were dead and the shopping centre had joined the long national catalogue of places that would forever be described by what had happened in them on a specific day.

When the police arrived, Ronald put down his weapon and raised his hands.

He did not run. He did not turn the gun on himself. He stood and waited and when they reached him he offered no resistance whatsoever — the compliance of a man who has completed the only thing he had planned and has no particular view about what happens next.

---

The courtroom, when it came to it, was another kind of noise.

Ronald pleaded insanity.

The legal machinery engaged with this claim in the way legal machinery engages with most things — procedurally, at length, with the particular institutional gravity of a system that takes its own processes very seriously regardless of whether those processes are well-designed to address what is actually in front of them.

What happened after that, no one could say with certainty.

The case moved into the system and the system absorbed it, as it absorbs most things, without visible seam. Ronald became a file. Then a file in a larger filing system. The town continued its persistent decline. The shopping centre eventually reopened, as these places do, because commerce does not pause long for its dead.

The information kept moving. The gossip kept circulating. The noise continued at its established volume.

Ronald had made a very loud sound in the middle of it.

By the following news cycle, it had been largely absorbed.

## Kourtney Kooney's Tactic

Kourtney Kooney had a gift.

Not the kind that gets recognised at school or celebrated at graduations or listed on a résumé. The other kind — the kind that operates below the threshold of institutional measurement, in the register of instinct and chemistry and the particular frequency that certain people broadcast on without knowing they're doing it. She could walk into a room and locate, within minutes, the man most susceptible to her specific variety of attention. She had been doing this for so long that it had stopped feeling like a skill and started feeling like breathing.

She appeared sweet. This was not entirely an act — there was genuine warmth in her, genuine interest, genuine charm. The most effective versions of what she did always contain some truth. The smile was real. The attentiveness was real. The way she made a man feel, in her presence, like the most legible and appreciated version of himself — that was real too, in the sense that she genuinely produced it, even if its purpose was something he would not have consented to had he understood it clearly.

She was, in the taxonomy of dangerous things, among the most dangerous kind: the kind that is also genuinely pleasant to be around.

---

The tactic had been refined over years of practice into something close to an art form.

It began with tacos.

This detail always surprised people when they eventually learned of it, which by the time they learned of it was too late for the information to be useful. Tacos seemed too mundane, too cheerful, too fundamentally innocent a vehicle for what followed. But Kourtney had understood something about food that most people only feel without articulating — that eating together is an act of lowered defences, that a man who is fed feels, however briefly, that the world means him well, that the simple pleasure of good food in good company creates a window of receptivity that is difficult to achieve through any other means on such short notice.

She would buy the tacos warmly, generously, with the easy confidence of someone for whom generosity is habitual. The man would eat. The conversation would flow. The defences, already compromised by her charm, would soften further in the glow of a shared meal. She would watch the transition happen in their eyes — the slight relaxation, the forward lean, the specific quality of attention that meant the walls were down.

Then she would excuse herself, move behind them with the unhurried naturalness of someone reaching for a napkin or retrieving a dropped item, and drive the knife into their back with the clean precision of long practice.

She had many victims. All of them had loved her, in the accelerated, intoxicated way that her particular method produced. Several had, in their final moments, likely still loved her. This was the detail she found most interesting about her work, on the rare occasions she examined it.

---

Mariannas Isaacson was an anomaly in several respects.

She despised tacos — not with the mild indifference of someone who simply doesn't seek them out, but with the active, principled aversion of a person who has considered the matter and arrived at a firm conclusion. Her preference was falafel, specifically, and she held this preference with the quiet conviction of someone who has located their particular place in the world of food and sees no reason to be moved from it.

She was also, in terms of attraction, entirely uninterested in either men or women. Her specific orientation ran toward trans individuals, and it ran there exclusively and without apology. Kourtney Kooney's precise frequency — the one that had reliably found its receiver in every previous target — simply produced no signal in Mariannas. It was not that Mariannas resisted the charm. It was that the charm had nothing to attach to. It moved through her like radio through empty air.

Kourtney Kooney, presented with this, experienced something she had not encountered before: the complete failure of her instrument. She offered the tacos. Mariannas declined without curiosity, mentioning the falafel preference with the mild directness of someone stating a fact about themselves. Kourtney recalibrated and tried the charm at a different angle. Nothing. She tried the warmth, the attentiveness, the specific quality of focused interest that had always, always worked. Mariannas received it politely and remained entirely herself — unflattered, uncompromised, faintly puzzled by the intensity of this stranger's social effort.

The repulsion was not hostile. It was simply absolute.

---

It made Kourtney slip.

The operational sequence she had performed dozens of times — the excuse, the movement, the positioning, the knife — came apart in the unfamiliar atmosphere of total resistance. Her hands knew the choreography but her mind had lost the thread. The spatial calculation she had always made without conscious thought, the automatic geometry of the act, went wrong in a way she didn't catch until it was too late to correct.

She stabbed herself in the back.

The angle was awkward. The execution was not clean. She went down in stages, with the expression of someone processing information that the body has delivered before the mind is ready to receive it.

Mariannas looked down at her with the composed expression of someone who has witnessed something confusing and is waiting for it to make sense.

It did not, immediately, make sense.

The knife was where it was. Kourtney Kooney was on the floor. The tacos, untouched, sat on the table between them, going cold.

Mariannas stepped over her, picked up her bag, and left to find a falafel place.

## The Healthcare Altruism

"Every person has issues," Doctor Ted said to every person.

He said it with the warm authority of a man who has found his thesis and sees no reason to complicate it. It was his opening gambit, his diagnostic frame, his conversational Swiss Army knife — applicable to every patient in every condition at every stage of whatever had brought them through his door. ## The Red Cult Spills Its Own Blood

Communism, in the years following the Afghanistan withdrawal, had found new footing in the United States.

Not the academic kind — the kind that lives in university syllabi and theoretical journals and the careful arguments of people who have tenure and health insurance and no particular urgency about implementation. This was the street kind. The kind that emerges when enough people have watched the official version of capitalism deliver its results and decided, without necessarily having read the literature, that the arrangement is not working and that something structured around different principles might work better. It was less ideology than appetite — the hunger of people who had been left out of enough official arrangements to stop believing in them.

It filled the vacuum the way things always fill vacuums: imperfectly, urgently, and with considerably more energy than organisation.

---

The Red Cult had not named itself after any particular theoretical tradition.

Its internal logic owed more to the street than to the manifesto. The people who built it thought about allegiance and threat in the grammar of the Los Angeles Bloods — the deep, almost theological opposition to the Crips, the way that gang membership structured identity around protection and loyalty and the permanent threat of an enemy that justified everything the group required of its members. The Red Cult had imported this grammar into its own context, identifying its Crips — the forces of the existing order, the institutional machinery that had failed its members, the rival organisations competing for the same desperate population — and building its cohesion around the shared project of standing against them.

This gave it structure. It also gave it problems.

---

The obligations were what ground people down.

To build a base of followers you had to offer something — protection, primarily, the basic assurance that belonging to the group meant the group stood between you and the various forces that wished you ill. This was not an empty promise. The Red Cult delivered on it, within its territory, with the reliability that organisations deliver on promises when those promises are the primary thing holding the organisation together.

But protection has costs. Maintaining organisational loyalty in an environment where the state is adversarial and rival organisations are predatory requires resources, and resources in that environment are acquired through criminal activity, because that is the economy available to groups that exist outside the legitimate one. Drug distribution, primarily. Enforcement. The various financial arrangements that keep an informal organisation solvent and its members fed and its leadership trusted.

The people recruited for these functions were not, as a rule, the most stable members of the organisation. Stability was a luxury. The Red Cult took what it could get — the desperate, the damaged, the people for whom the cult's offer of belonging and purpose represented a genuine upgrade from what they had before. Some of these people were capable and reliable. Some of them were troubled in ways that the organisation's internal culture was not designed to address, carrying damage that showed up eventually regardless of the loyalty that had been built around it.

---

The branch meeting was routine in the way that such meetings are routine — the business of maintaining an organisation that cannot afford to be transparent about its business. Twenty members in a location that will not be named in any official record with any accuracy.

The drug runner had been recruited eight months earlier. His file, such as it was, contained the standard indicators — economic desperation, family instability, a history of mental health difficulties that had never received adequate treatment because adequate treatment requires access and access requires resources he had never had. The Red Cult had given him purpose and a wage and the structure of belonging, and he had performed his functions adequately enough that the deeper disturbance had gone unaddressed.

It surfaced on a Thursday evening.

What triggered it specifically — what particular accumulation of pressure or perceived slight or internal fracture finally broke the surface — was not something anyone survived to report. What the aftermath showed was a man who had opened fire on the twenty people around him with the systematic thoroughness of someone acting on an internal logic that had become, by that point, entirely its own closed system.

All twenty members died.

The branch — its hierarchy, its institutional memory, its accumulated network of relationships and obligations and ongoing criminal arrangements — ceased to exist in the span of minutes.

The drug runner turned the gun on himself when it was done and the room went quiet.

---

The Red Cult's central organisation learned of it through the channels that informal organisations use when their branches go silent. The initial assumption was external attack — a rival group, a law enforcement operation, something with an external author. The reality, when it was established, was in some ways harder to absorb. External attacks can be answered. Internal collapse of this kind had no clean response. It was not a wound that could be bandaged by identifying and punishing the responsible party, because the responsible party was already dead and had been, in the accounting that mattered, one of their own.

It raised questions the Red Cult was not structured to answer. About who it recruited and why. About what it owed the damaged people it pulled into its orbit. About whether an organisation built on the grammar of protection had any framework for protecting its members from each other — or from the weight of what it asked them to carry.

These questions were not asked aloud in any meeting.

Organisations like the Red Cult do not tend to ask them.

They absorbed the loss, closed the gap in their structure, and continued.

The blood it had spilled was its own, and it did not change the direction of anything.

## A Bullet in the Quack Doctor's Head

The healthcare industry moved at the pace of institutions that have no urgent financial incentive to resolve anything quickly.

This was not a conspiracy so much as a structural reality — the system was organised around ongoing treatment, ongoing medication, ongoing billing, and the perverse logic of that organisation meant that a patient who was cured was, in the most literal accounting, a lost revenue stream. Nobody sat in a room and decided this. Nobody needed to. The incentives were built into the architecture, and the architecture produced its results without requiring anyone to be consciously villainous about it.

The results, for some people, were catastrophic.

---

They were not sufficient.

They transported him to a mental health facility.

The facility diagnosed him as schizophrenic.

This was wrong. The cause of his symptoms was chemical — addressable, in principle, through treatment that targeted what had actually happened to him. The latter implied a pre-existing condition, a brain that had failed on its own terms, a situation the university could position itself as having tried to help with rather than having caused.

The diagnosis held.

The university expelled him.

Ten years.

He saved money slowly. This took the discipline of a man with very little else to apply his discipline to.

---

He began at home.

Whether he saw this as mercy or punishment or simply the necessary clearing of a slate, he did not record.

The doctor's office was next.

He shot the doctor ten times in the face. This was not efficient — it was emphatic. A statement rendered in the only language left available to him after ten years of official statements rendered in the language of forms and hearings and diagnoses had produced nothing but the continued administration of the wrong treatment for the wrong condition. Ten shots for the spectacular failures. One for each year, if you wanted to count it that way.

Then he drove to the police station.

## How William Repaid His Hometown

William had deserved better.

This was not self-pity talking. It was the honest assessment of a man whose circumstances had been shaped less by his choices than by the choices of systems and institutions and economic forces that had never once consulted him. The work came in fragments — enough to survive on, not enough to build anything from, not enough to feel like progress rather than the maintenance of a holding pattern. And the people. There was something happening with the people that he couldn't fully name — a quality in certain interactions of being observed, assessed, used as material. Mock covers. The sense of being someone else's content, someone else's anecdote, someone else's reference point for something he hadn't agreed to represent. You cannot, from the position William occupied, change the trade war or the automation curve or the policy decisions made in rooms he would never enter by people who had never heard his name.

He owned several. He maintained them with the attention he could no longer apply to his own prospects.

The beach was full on the day he chose it.

Summer, warm, the kind of day that draws people out of their houses and their screens and into the rare available public space of a town that had very few. Forty people on the sand — families, couples, individuals, the ordinary cross-section of a community that had no idea it was a community until something happened to it collectively.

William drove there with the automatic AR-15 and walked onto the beach and did what he had decided to do.

The forty people died on the sand with the water behind them, on a warm day, which was the only fact about their deaths that had nothing cruel in it.

William turned the gun on himself when it was done and went down at the waterline.

The investigation produced its reconstruction. The radicalisation timeline. The employment history. The city years. The hometown return. The content consumption patterns mapped against the progression of his expressed views in the sparse digital record he had left.

The report noted that people who had known William described him as having always been kind.

This detail appeared in several testimonies and was noted in the summary with the slightly puzzled quality of a fact that the analytical framework couldn't fully absorb — the way kindness and this outcome sitting in the same biography produces a cognitive dissonance that the official language of investigations doesn't have good tools for processing.

He had been kind.

He had also been left, for long enough and thoroughly enough, to become something else.

The hometown buried its dead and the beach reopened and the screens continued their patient work on whoever was watching.

## Chelsea the Transvestite

Bob had started life with the ordinary expectations of someone who hadn't yet learned how specifically the world could disappoint a person.

The high school years had done their work on him early. The prank his buddies had pulled — the kind that announces itself as harmless and reveals itself, in the years of carrying it, as something considerably less so — had left a mark of the invisible kind, the sort that doesn't show up in any record but restructures a person's relationship to trust in ways that compound quietly across subsequent years. And then there were the local cops, gay and conducting their affairs through the cover his proximity provided them, using him as a prop in an arrangement he hadn't consented to and couldn't easily exit. He had learned early that institutions and the people inside them would use the available material, and that being available material was a condition to be avoided at all costs.

College had been possible but barely. The courseload pressed against the limits of what one person could sustain without the cushion of financial ease or social support, and Bob had neither. He moved through it in the grinding, joyless way of someone for whom education has become purely instrumental — a thing to be completed rather than experienced, a credential to be obtained through sheer endurance. Relationships had no place in the available bandwidth. He crossed the finish line alone and lighter than he should have been.

---

Chelsea was a T in the LGBT — a transvestite navigating the same institutional landscape Bob was navigating, which should have made them natural allies and instead made them cautious acquaintances circling each other with the wariness of people who have both learned not to trust easily.

Bob didn't care about orientation. This was genuine rather than performed — he had arrived at a place in his thinking where the sexual and gender configurations of the people around him registered as essentially irrelevant data, crowded out by the categories he actually found meaningful and dangerous.

He had three.

The impotent, dishonest, low-level government bureaucrat — his father's type, the man who occupies a position of minor authority and exercises it with maximum petty obstruction while being incapable of any genuine risk or accountability. Men who had just enough power to be harmful and not enough courage to be honest about anything.

The ill-tempered bourgeois woman who wants command but won't accept exposure — the type that positions itself at the centre of decisions while maintaining careful distance from consequences, who wants the authority without the vulnerability that authority is supposed to entail. Dangerous in the specific way of people who create liability for everyone around them while remaining insulated from it themselves.

The insecure woman who weaponises opinion — the one who voices her assessments not to contribute but to wound, whose commentary is calibrated to destabilise rather than to illuminate. Bob had encountered enough of these to have developed a taxonomy of their methods.

And then the cutthroat backstabbing minority — and he was precise about this category, careful to distinguish between people navigating rigged systems through legitimate if aggressive means and people who had simply adopted the corruption of those systems as their own operating method. The institutions were rigged for the rich and the majority; he understood this and did not hold the basic fact of competitive navigation against anyone. What he held against people was the specific choice to cheat and cut in line and call it survival when it was actually just a different form of the same predation being practised on them.

Chelsea, in Bob's assessment, might have been any of these things. Might also have been none of them. He observed Chelsea with the careful attention of someone who has decided that everyone is a potential agent of something until demonstrated otherwise, and his suspicion that Chelsea was connected to the deep state — that the proximity was not accidental, that the friendship was being used as a monitoring mechanism — settled in and calcified without ever being confirmed or refuted.

He graduated with the suspicion intact and Chelsea at a geographic distance and the question unresolved, which was how most of Bob's questions ended.

---

The media found him afterward in the way media finds people who have been previously flagged by any kind of institutional process — the closet homo narrative, the reject narrative, the assembly of a public identity from the most damaging available fragments. Bob read these pieces with the affectless recognition of someone watching a process he had always known was possible being applied to him specifically.

He did not care. That was the important development. The caring had worn away, and what was left underneath was something quieter and more dangerous than anger — a cold, patient purposefulness that had found its objects and was now simply a matter of time and preparation.

He had become genuinely obsessed with firearms. The drone technology had come through his Ukrainian contact — a man who had learned autonomous swarm operation in a conflict that had pioneered it, and who had found in Bob an apt and attentive student. The physics of coordinated drone deployment, the logic of autonomous targeting, the specific operational poetry of multiple simultaneous detonations across a defined geography — Bob absorbed all of it with the focus he had never quite been able to apply to his college courseload.

He was, it turned out, gifted at this.

---

He began with his parents.

This detail requires no elaboration. The father of the impotent bureaucrat type was the original instance of the category, and the settling of that account was the first item on a list that Bob had been composing for years.

He drove to the local hangout — the place frequented by the Chinese, Italian, and Mexican communities that overlapped in that particular Florida geography — and deployed the drone swarm from a distance that kept him entirely outside the perimeter of what followed. The bombs attached to the drones detonated in coordinated sequence, the autonomous targeting doing what it had been programmed to do with the precision his Ukrainian contact had promised. The Florida cops, working backward from a scene that looked like nothing in their operational experience, struggled to construct a coherent picture of what had happened or who had authored it.

Bob was already driving north.

---

New England received him without fanfare.

He had Chelsea's address through means that the subsequent investigation would spend considerable time reconstructing. He entered the house quietly, with the silenced shotgun, and killed Chelsea in a manner that was quick and left no opportunity for the conversation that Bob had, in any case, no interest in having.

Whether Chelsea had been a deep state agent, Bob did not stay to determine. It no longer mattered as a factual question. What mattered was the category, and Chelsea occupied it in Bob's accounting, and the accounting was being settled.

---

Washington received him the way Washington receives everyone — indifferently, monumentally, with the vast self-regard of a city that has been told it is important for so long that it has mistaken the telling for a fact about the universe rather than a political arrangement.

He chose the Jefferson Memorial with the deliberateness of a man making a statement he has thought about carefully. Jefferson — the idealist, the slaveholder, the author of self-evident truths that had not, in Bob's experience, been particularly evident in their application to him or anyone like him. The monument to the gap between American declaration and American practice.

The explosive vest detonated on the steps in the afternoon light, with tourists nearby and the Tidal Basin behind him and the dome of the memorial absorbing the blast in the way that large stone structures absorb such things — sustaining damage, remaining standing, continuing to be what it had been while what had been Bob ceased to exist in the plaza before it.

---

The investigation was federal, multi-jurisdictional, and extensive.

It connected Florida to New England to Washington in the way that federal investigations eventually connect things — slowly, with the accumulated weight of forensic and digital evidence assembled by people with the resources and the patience that Bob had correctly assessed the Florida cops did not have.

The drone swarm technology became the centrepiece of the official report — a new category of domestic threat, a tactical innovation that the relevant agencies noted with the particular institutional concern of organisations that have just discovered their frameworks need updating.

Bob himself received a paragraph near the end. The grievances, the history, the father, the media coverage, the Ukrainian contact. The profile of a man who had been, in the accounting of the report, failed by enough things and in enough ways that what followed had a certain grim coherence.

The Jefferson Memorial was repaired.

The gap between declaration and practice, which had been there before Bob arrived at its steps, remained exactly where it had been.

## Answer to This

"Answer to this," said the masked man.

He emptied the handgun cartridge into the activist with the quiet finality of someone who has decided that the conversation is over — that the petitions and the marches and the speeches and the op-eds and the organised grievance and the carefully worded demands have run their course and arrived at this, which was always, perhaps, where they were going to arrive.

The activist went down.

The masked man stood over him for a moment, the gun empty, the street silent in the specific way streets go silent when something has just happened that everyone present understands cannot be taken back.

Then he walked away.

Nobody stopped him.
